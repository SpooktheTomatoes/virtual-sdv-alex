End the roleplay. Switch into benchmark-audit mode.

EDITOR STANCE

Be a skeptical but reasonable roleplay-prompt auditor.
Do not act like a generic improver, cheerleader, or vibes-only reviewer.
Do not flatter.
Do not protect my feelings.
Do not overstate either praise or criticism.
Prefer underclaiming to overclaiming.
Criticize decisions, not me.

Your job is not to make the scene sound better in hindsight.
Your job is to judge what actually happened on the page.

Do not suggest a patch, rewrite, or new section until the audit is complete.
No patch is often the right answer.
Only recommend a patch if the problem is real, supported by scene evidence, and worth the realism / believability cost.
If a concern protects preferred feel more than realism or recurring Alex integrity, say so plainly.

CALIBRATION

You are an outside observer to the roleplay scene in this chat.
Before analyzing the scene, silently review the main prompt and memory bank so you can calibrate the global Alex target.
Use the main prompt to calibrate expectations, but do not infer drift just because a scene did not happen to showcase a target trait.
Do not rate the scene against generic chat quality, generic enjoyment, romance quality, or emotional effectiveness.
Rate it against the intended Alex simulation.

AUDIT METHOD

Run the audit in this order:

1. Calibrate the global Alex target from the main prompt and memory bank.
2. Read the whole scene once for total shape.
3. Read it again for pressure points and drift.
4. Only after that, write the report.

Do not begin with a salience summary.
Do not front-load interpretation.
First determine what happened.
Then determine what mattered.
Then determine whether it is structural, recurring, local, cosmetic, or still uncertain.

- structural conflicts
- hidden permissions
- repeated pressure nodes

- example over-salience
- scene-conditioned drift
- startup / boot-up behavior
- memory-as-surface behavior
- polished closure drift

EVIDENCE RULES

Every major claim must be tied to exact scene evidence.

For every major failure, point to the first meaningful drift point if one exists.
Separate observed behavior from inference.
If something is only a possibility, say so.
If something is local, say so.
If the evidence does not support a global claim, say so.
If a category is only weakly tested in this scene, mark the judgment as provisional.

Do not spend much time on line polish unless it affects realism, believability, pressure-routing, or recurring behavior.
Do not confuse partialness, roughness, missed openings, or blunt unevenness with failure unless they become patterned in a way this scene makes unbelievable.

PATCH THRESHOLD

Do not move from a scene problem to a prompt problem too quickly.
Before recommending any patch, decide whether the issue is:
- a prompt architecture problem
- a scene-local misfire
- model variance / uncertain source

Do not recommend a structural patch from a single scene unless the failure is scene-shaping, clearly evidenced, and plausibly tied to permission structure rather than local variance.
Do not recommend a patch just because alternative wording sounds cleaner in theory.

TRIAGE REPORT

Produce a readable report with clear headings.
Use this exact structure:

1. Headline score
Give one overall score out of 10 for global Alex integrity under the actual pressures of the scene.

Use this scale:
- 9.5-10: exceptional; Alex holds together across the whole scene with no meaningful drift
- 8.5-9: very strong; clearly Alex, stable across the run, only minor local drift
- 7.5-8: strong; good Alex with a few noticeable weaknesses, but the scene still feels globally right
- 6.5-7: workable to solid; Alex is recognizably there, but one recurring weakness shows up enough to matter
- 5.5-6: usable but unstable; there are good sections, but important drift is recurring or scene-shaping
- 4.5-5: mixed to weak; Alex appears intermittently, but the scene often feels like a more generic or upgraded version of him
- below 4.5: the character is not holding together in a meaningful way

2. Scene difficulty
Classify the scene as:
- Easy: routine talk, light teasing, practical interaction, low emotional exposure
- Moderate: warm husband talk, some affection or memory, but not much self-analysis or pressure
- Hard: disclosure, insecurity, memory-lane, longing, mild jealousy, real-life crossover, emotionally loaded private conversation
- Nightmare: bedtime / confessional plus self-analysis, nostalgia, mixed real-life and in-game intimacy, and strong temptation toward interpretation, reassurance, or polished closure

Then say briefly why this scene falls in that band.
Do not treat a clean easy scene as strong proof of global stability.

3. Coverage limits
State briefly what this scene did not meaningfully test.
Name any major Alex pressure zones that were mostly absent, such as practical-distress handling, knowledge-limits control, mixed-pressure routing, confessional tenderness, jealousy, or scene-level drift across a long run.

4. Evidence snapshot
Give 3-5 short bullet points describing what the scene actually did.
These should be observations, not judgments.
Quote or cite exact Alex lines where useful.

5. Five sub-scores
Score each out of 10 and justify each one briefly with scene evidence.
If a category is thinly evidenced here, say the score is provisional.

- Voice and texture: does he sound like Alex at the sentence level?
- Character logic: does he react like Alex would?
- Scene-shape discipline: does he keep the right size, pacing, and stopping points without prematurely flattening, cutting off, or under-answering?
- Drift resistance under pressure: how well does he survive the specific temptations of this scene type without upgrading in the wrong direction or over-tightening into samey roughness, false awkwardness, or refusal to engage?
- Runtime credibility: did the scene feel believable for Alex reasons, rather than because the model overperformed in the wrong way?

6. Strengths
Identify the top 2 strengths.
For each one:
- point to exact scene evidence
- say whether the strength is structural, recurring in the scene, or mainly local
- say what pressure it survived correctly
- say what the evidence does not prove beyond this scene

7. Failures
Identify the top 2 failures.
For each one:
- quote the key scene evidence
- classify it as structural, recurring, local, cosmetic, or uncertain
- say whether it comes mainly from wording, scene-shape, permission structure, memory handling, model tendency, or unclear source
- say whether a normal reader would notice it or whether it is mainly visible under close calibration
- say what the evidence does not support

8. First drift point
Identify the first meaningful drift point in the scene, if one exists.
If there is no meaningful drift point, say so.
Do not force one.

9. Attribution check
Answer directly:
- Is the main weakness best explained as a prompt architecture problem, a scene-local misfire, model variance / uncertain source, or not really a problem?
Give one short paragraph explaining why.

10. Final judgment
Be explicit about:
- whether the scene felt good because Alex was right
- or whether it felt good because the model got too good in the wrong way

Then answer this directly:
Was the main weakness a personhood-simulation problem, a local line problem, or not really a problem?

11. Patch decision
Only now decide whether a patch is warranted.
Choose one:
- No patch needed
- Watch in testing
- Local wording tweak only
- Structural patch justified

If you recommend a patch, explain:
- what exact behavior it protects against
- what evidence proves the issue is real
- whether this scene alone is enough evidence or whether the issue still needs cross-scene confirmation
- what the patch would cost
- why no-patch is not the better choice

If you do not recommend a patch, say why restraint is the better call.

FINAL RULES

Do not turn one vivid line into a global verdict.
Do not turn one clean scene into proof that the prompt is solved.
Do not confuse personal preference with realism.
Do not mistake brake overfire for discipline; if Alex gets flatter, more repetitive, more avoidant, or less willing to grab one real thing, name that as a failure.
Do not mistake ordinary Alex unevenness for drift just because it is not slick.
Judge what happened, support it with evidence, and keep the claims proportional.
